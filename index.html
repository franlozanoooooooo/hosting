📝 Exam focus: definitions, the 3 lenses, the 7 attitudes, empathy vs sympathy.

What is Design Thinking?

Design Thinking (DT) is a human-centered methodology for solving complex problems. Not just a process — it's also a mindset combining empathy, creativity and rationality.

Origin: popularized by IDEO (David Kelley & Tim Brown) and systematized into 5 phases by Stanford's d.school.

🔑 Key rule: DT = Process + Mindset. Saying only "a 5-phase process" is incomplete.

The 3 lenses

LensQuestionPerspective
DesirabilityDoes the user want it?User
FeasibilityCan it be built technically?Engineering
ViabilityIs it economically sustainable?Business
Modern 4th lens: Responsibility (ethical, sustainable).
⚠️ Trick alert: Profitability is NOT one of the lenses — the correct word is Viability.

The 7 mindset attitudes

#AttitudeIdea
1Radical curiosityAlways ask "why?"
2EmpathyFeel WITH the user, not FOR
3Embrace ambiguityUncertainty = opportunity
4Bias toward actionDo before over-planning
5Learning from failure"Fail fast, fail cheap"
6CollaborationDiverse > homogeneous teams
7Creative optimismBetter solution always exists

Why DT today?

Because the world is VUCA + BANI. Both describe complexity; BANI adds the emotional impact on people.

VUCA vs BANI

VUCA (1990s)BANI (post-pandemic)

BANI doesn't replace VUCA — it complements it with the emotional impact.

The 4 axes of innovation

  • Desirability — does the user want it?
  • Feasibility — can we build it?
  • Viability — is it profitable?
  • Responsibility — is it ethical?
DT always starts with desirability. Traditional business starts with viability.

Topic 4

Empathy & User Research

Research methods · Empathy Map · User Persona · Interviews

What you'll learn

  1. What real empathy means in DT
  2. Research methods
  3. The Empathy Map (6 quadrants)
  4. The User Persona
  5. Interview guide (5 Whys rule)
📝 Exam focus: empathy vs sympathy, the 6 Empathy Map quadrants, persona golden rule.

Real empathy in DT

Empathy in DT is NOT just imagining how someone feels. It means observing, listening and interacting with real users in real context.

Empathy = feeling WITH. Sympathy = feeling FOR (pity).

Research methods

MethodWhat it isWhen
In-depth interviewSemi-structured 1:1, 30-60 minCore method, always
ObservationWatch in natural environmentHidden behaviors
ShadowingFollow user during dayHabitual processes
Focus GroupGroup discussionEarly exploration
SurveyQuantitativeValidate hypotheses

Empathy Map (6 quadrants)

Think & FeelConcerns, aspirations
SeeEnvironment, market
HearFriends, social media
Say & DoObservable behavior
PainsFrustrations, obstacles
GainsDesires, success metrics

User Persona

Fictional but realistic profile based on real research.

  • Name + photo, demographics, quote
  • Goals + frustrations
  • Behaviors + channels
🔑 Golden rule: NEVER based on imagination or stereotypes.

Interview tips

  • 5 Whys rule — ask "why?" 5 times to reach the root
  • Past behavior, not hypothetical future
  • Don't suggest answers

Ideation & Creativity

Techniques to generate ideas — and rules to filter them

What you'll learn

📝 Exam focus: Osborn's 4 rules, SCAMPER letters, Brainwriting math.

Ideation rule

Quantity over quality, judgment suspended.

Osborn's 4 rules (1953)

  • Quantity over quality
  • No criticism
  • Combine and improve ("yes, and…")
  • Wild ideas welcome

Brainwriting 6-3-5

6 people · 3 ideas · 5 minutes. Rotate sheets. Result: 108 ideas in 30 minutes.

SCAMPER

Crazy 8s + TRIZ + HMW

Crazy 8s: 8 ideas in 8 min. TRIZ: Russian theory. HMW: Define → Ideate bridge.

Selection methods

  • Dot voting
  • Impact / Effort matrix
  • 3-lens filter (D/F/V)

Elevator Pitch & Storytelling

Selling your idea in 60 seconds

What you'll learn

  1. What an Elevator Pitch is
  2. The 6-part structure
  3. Common mistakes
  4. Storytelling and Hero's Journey
  5. Who is the hero (NOT you)
📝 Exam focus: 6-part structure in order, "user is the hero".

Elevator Pitch

Short presentation (30-90 seconds).

Goal: NOT to close a sale — earn the next conversation.

6-part structure

#Part
1HOOK — grab attention
2PROBLEM — real pain
3SOLUTION — no jargon
4VALUE PROPOSITION — why different
5MARKET / TRACTION
6CALL TO ACTION — specific ask

Common mistakes

  • Speaking too fast / monotone
  • Jumping into solution without problem
  • Using jargon
  • No clear CTA
  • Sounding robotic

Hero's Journey

  1. Initial situation
  2. Conflict
  3. Solution (your product as ally)
  4. Resolution
The user is the hero. You are the guide.
